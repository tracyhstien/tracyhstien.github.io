Analyzing Agricultural Outcomes in Iowa

By Tracy Tien, Smith College Spatial Analysis Lab

Introduction1

In our explorations of the history of agricultural land use and its impacts on environmental and human health, we are reading about the changes to farming in the U.S. including the shift from smaller family farms that produced a variety of crops, to large industrial monocultures. This raises the question: What are the adverse environmental and cultural impacts and how are these impacts distributed among disadvantaged communities? The US Midwest, “America’s Breadbasket,” is especially tantillizing in terms of environmental degradation due to farming.2

In anticipation for this lab, we reviewed the Soils Reveal Project for soil carbon loss in Iowa. These maps display soil carbon losses due to land use practices. Industrial agricultural practices such as tilling and reliance on heavy use of fertilizer and pesticide have resulted in a loss of carbon storage in the soil and its release into the atmosphere where it contributes to planetary warming. Carbon emissions are further increased due to the shift from production of food intended directly for human consumption and feed for livestock and industrial food products such as corn syrup and other by-products of corn including ethanol.

These exercises are intended to allow you to use various data layers to analyze and visualize these issues in a GIS (Geographic Information System) using ArcGIS Pro.

Some initial guiding questions are:

  1. What evidence do we see that industrial agriculture leads to adverse environmental impacts such as reduced soil carbon storage and declines in surface water quality? Do crop types and farm sizes differ?
  2. Where are the industrial food processing facilities (concentrated animal feeding operations (CAFOs) located? Do these locations coincide with environmental justice communities?

CAFOs and EJ Communities

Problem Statement & Rationale

How can we illustrate the ways in which industrial food processing, such as concentrated animal feeding operations (CAFOs), place additional burdens on environmental justice communities?

To contextualize our workflow, we need to define and propose our criteria:

EJ communities
Under Executive Order 12898, within the environmental justice framework, socially vulnerable communities are low-income and minority.4 EJ communities are census block groups.
Living 3 miles from a CAFO increases respiratory health issues
This study suggests: “residential proximity to a CAFO was associated with reduced lung function and self-reported asthma at a distance within 3-4 miles.”

Our workflow:

  • Use the EJScreen layer to show where census block groups that are both low-income and minority
  • Analyze the animal feeding operations layer to delineate areas that are within 3 miles
  • Use the areas that are within 3 miles from a CAFO to find which EJ communities are within

Query Data

As you can see, the EJScreen data are of the contiguous US and Alaska, but we want to focus on Iowa. So, we want to create a subset of the EJScreen layer that is of Iowa.

  1. In Contents, right-click on the EJSCREEN_Full layer, and find Properties at the bottom of the drop-down
  2. Under the Definition Query tab >> + New definition query
    • The expression is: Where STATE_NAME is equal to Iowa. Click Apply >> OK
  3. In Contents, with EJSCREEN_Full layer selected, click once to rename the layer to EJSCREEN_Iowa
lightbulb_outline To zoom to the full extent of the layer you're working with, right-click >> Zoom to Layer.

Symbolizing Data

To show the EJ communities in Iowa, we need to show where the census block groups are both low-income and minority. We will take two different approaches and consider how effective each are.

Approach #1: Choropleth Map Using a Single Variable

The term “choropleth” comes from Greek “choro” (area) and “plethos” (multitude). A choropleth map thus uses a color gradient to show quantities over a geographic area.

This means we should choose one variable depicted within the EJScreen data to apply this graduated symbology.

  1. Right-click on EJSCREEN_Iowa, then Open Attribute Table, scroll across to examine what information is available in this layer
    2. info_outline As you scroll across, the columns (referred to as “fields” in GIS) are plenty and with cryptic names like “ACSTOTPOP.” By referencing the EJSCREEN Technical Documentation, you can figure out what each field means. (ACSTOTPOP means American Community Survey Total Population)
  2. We will use “P_VULEOPCT”, which is the numeric Demographic Index = (% minority + % low-income)/25
  3. Right-click on EJSCREEN_Iowa >> Symbology. This will prompt the Symbology pane to open on the right side.
    • Under Primary symbology, choose Graduated Colors
    • Next under Field, select “P_VULEOPCT”
    • Under Color Scheme, choose “Oranges (continuous)”
info_outline Because we're depicting one variable, best practice is to use a single color gradient to represent that intuitively.

This map shows that, under the environmental justice framework, the census block groups in Iowa from less socially vulnerable (light orange) to more vulnerable (deep orange).

Approach #2: Bivariate Choropleth Map

Socioeconomic status (like income) is intricately intertwined with certain race and ethnicity (minority)6 – so we would expect that the two variables, low-income and minority, are somewhat related. This is why we can use bivariate mapping to show where the two variables are in agreement or not.

  1. Right-click on EJSCREEN_Iowa >> Copy, then immediately above (still in Contents), under Map, right-click to Paste
    • Rename this layer EJSCREEN_Full_Bivariate
  2. Open Symbology for EJSCREEN_Full_Bivariate
    • Under Primary symbology, choose Bivariate colors
    • For Field 1, choose “P_MINORPCT” and Field 2, “P_LWINPCT”

This map shows where minority population (% of total population within the census block group) are in the high percentile compared to the rest of the country (pink), where low income population (%) are in the high percentile (blue), and where there are least minority + low-income (grey) and most minority + low-income (purple).

Compare the Two Symbology Approaches

  2. Move down to the map view, and an arrow will appear, click anywhere and drag up & down to compare the two approaches to visualize EJ communities in Iowa
