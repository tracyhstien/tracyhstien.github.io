Analyzing Agricultural Outcomes in Iowa

By Tracy Tien, Smith College Spatial Analysis Lab in consultation with Denise Lello

Introduction1

In our explorations of the history of agricultural land use and its impacts on environmental and human health, we are reading about the changes to farming in the U.S. including the shift from smaller family farms that produced a variety of crops, to large industrial monocultures. This raises the question: What are the adverse environmental and cultural impacts and how are these impacts distributed among disadvantaged communities? The US Midwest, “America’s Breadbasket,” is especially tantillizing in terms of environmental degradation due to farming.2

In anticipation for this lab, we reviewed the Soils Reveal Project for soil carbon loss in Iowa. These maps display soil carbon losses due to land use practices. Industrial agricultural practices such as tilling and reliance on heavy use of fertilizer and pesticide have resulted in a loss of carbon storage in the soil and its release into the atmosphere where it contributes to planetary warming. Carbon emissions (not incorporatd in the Soils Revealed models) are further increased due to the shift from production of food intended directly for human consumption and feed for livestock and industrial food products such as corn syrup and other by-products of corn including ethanol.

These exercises are intended to allow you to use various data layers to analyze and visualize these issues in a GIS (Geographic Information System) using ArcGIS Pro.

Some of the large questions we would like to investigate, based on our course explanations are:

  1. Are factors such as crop type or farm size associated with different levels of impact on soil carbon storage or water quality?
  2. Can we detect environmental justice issues in the sitings of concentrated animal feeding operations (CAFOs)

CAFOs and EJ Communities

Problem Statement & Rationale

How can we illustrate which concentrated animal feeding operations (CAFOs) place additional burdens on environmental justice communities?

To contextualize our workflow, we need to define and propose our criteria:

EJ communities
Under Executive Order 12898, within the environmental justice framework, socially vulnerable communities are identified by the relative percents of low income and minority households, within them, the percentages of two factors averaged together.4 The units for EJ communities are census block groups.
Living 3 miles from a CAFO increases respiratory health issues
This study suggests: “residential proximity to a CAFO was associated with reduced lung function and self-reported asthma at a distance within 3-4 miles.”

Our workflow:

  • Use the EJScreen layer to show where census block groups that are both low-income and minority
  • Analyze the animal feeding operations layer to delineate areas that are within 3 miles
  • Use the areas that are within 3 miles from a CAFO to find which EJ communities are impacted

Symbolizing Data

To show the EJ communities in Iowa, we need to show where the census block groups are both low-income and minority. We will take two different approaches and consider how effective each are.

Approach #1: Choropleth Map Using a Single Variable

The term “choropleth” comes from Greek “choro” (area) and “plethos” (multitude). A choropleth map thus uses a color gradient to show quantities over a geographic area.

This means we should choose one variable depicted within the EJScreen data to apply this graduated symbology.

  1. Right-click on EJSCREEN_Iowa, then Open Attribute Table, scroll across to examine what information is available in this layer
    2. info_outline As you scroll across, the columns (referred to as “fields” in GIS) are plenty and with cryptic names like “ACSTOTPOP.” By referencing the EJSCREEN Technical Documentation, you can figure out what each field means. (ACSTOTPOP means American Community Survey Total Population)
  2. We will use “P_VULEOPCT”, which is the numeric percentile Demographic Index = (% minority + % low-income)/25
  3. Right-click on EJSCREEN_Iowa >> Symbology. This will prompt the Symbology pane to open on the right side.
    • Under Primary symbology, choose Graduated Colors
    • Next under Field, select “P_VULEOPCT”
    • Under Color Scheme, choose “Oranges (continuous)”

info_outline Because we're depicting one variable, best practice is to use a single color gradient to represent that intuitively.

Under the environmental justice framework, this map shows the census block groups in Iowa from less socially vulnerable (light orange) to more vulnerable (deep orange).

warning Save save your map.

Approach #2: Bivariate Choropleth Map

Socioeconomic status (like income) is intricately intertwined with certain race and ethnicity (minority)6 – so we would expect that the two variables, low-income and minority, are somewhat related. This is why we can use bivariate mapping to show where the two variables are in agreement or not.

  1. Right-click on EJSCREEN_Iowa >> Copy, then immediately above (still in Contents), under Map, right-click to Paste
    • Rename this layer EJSCREEN_Iowa_Bivariate
  2. Open Symbology for EJSCREEN_Iowa_Bivariate
    • Under Primary symbology, choose Bivariate colors
    • For Field 1, choose “P_MINORPCT” and Field 2, “P_LWINPCT”

This map shows where minority population (% of total population within the census block group) are in the high percentile compared to the rest of the country (pink), where low income population (%) are in the high percentile (blue), and where there are least minority + low-income (grey) and most minority + low-income (purple).

info_outline EJSCREEN uses percentile to contextualize the value within the rest of the nation. In our example, if the P_VULEOPCT of a census block group is 75, in the 75th percentile, this means only 25% of the rest of the nation are at the same level or higher of social vulnerability.

6. Environmental Degradation due to Agricultural Practices

6.1 Problem Statement & Rationale

What impacts do factors such as crop type or farm size have on soil carbon storage and water quality?

To contextualize our workflow, we need to define and propose our criteria:

Crop type
The agricultural land use by field layer is transformed with the predominant land use/land cover cropland data to distinguish the farm fields into four categories: (human) edible crop (e.g. sweet corn, pea), feed crop (e.g. triticale, alfalfa), non-agriculture (e.g. wetlands, forests), and other (e.g. clover.)
Farm size
We will categorize farm size into small, medium, and large based on acreage, using the same data classification scheme we used to bin the EJ community percentile last week: Jenks Natural Breaks. This method emphasizes variance between classes (distinct breaks in the distribution of data.)
Soil carbon change
The soil organic carbon on croplands and potential soil organic carbon on croplands after 20 years of medium scenario layers are rasters, meaning the layers portray in each grid/pixel, the values of soil organic carbon in tons per hectare. Since we have two time periods - 2014 and 2034 - we can calculate the difference in soil carbon storage to quantify change over time. The future projection on potential SOC means that the higher the difference, there’s less potential loss.
Water quality change
Similar to soil carbon change, we need two time periods to demonstrate change in water quality. We will use nitrate testing results in private well water for 2010 and 2020. In particular, we’re looking at the percent change in elevated nitrate tests (>= 10 mg/L). This means if the difference between the two is positive, the water quality became worse; while if the value is negative, the water quality improved.
Unit of analysis
The water quality data is reported at the county level. In order to make comparisons, our derived insights are aggregated to Iowa counties.

Our workflow:

First, we’ll breakdown the problem statement into digestible parts as it contains multiple questions. We will start with the following: Does crop type have an impact on soil carbon storage? The processes below can then be replicated for any other combination (i.e. crop type vs. water quality, farm size vs. SOC…etc.)

  • Analyze the agricultural land use by (farm) fields layer to calculate
    and show the composition of crop type for each county
  • Create a new data layer that is the difference (the change) between the current and future soil organic carbon value by: Δ = t2 - t1
  • Calculate the average (mean) change for each county

6.3 Tabulate Intersection

The IowaFieldBoundaries2019 layer contains individual farm fields in Iowa. Open Attribute Table to see the information that would be useful for us, such as “Acres” and “d_MAJORITY” (crop type). We want to know for each county, the area and percentage of each crop type. We use Tabulate Intersection to compute these two intersecting layers.

  1. On the top of the interface, click on the View tab >> Geoprocessing window
  2. Search tabulate intersection and double-click on the tool
  3. In the tool window:
    • Input Zone Features: IowaCounties
    • Zone Fields: CountyName
    • Input Class Features: IowaFieldBoundaries2019
    • Output Table: name the output file TabulateCropType
    • Class Fields: d_MAJORITY
    • Output Units: Acres
    • Click Run
    4. info_outline Note that our output table is saved to our project .gdb, which is a geodatabase - a spatial folder that can accommodate various spatial data formats.
  4. Once the geoprocessing finishes, the output table TabulateCropType will show up under Contents. Right-click to open the table. You will see that the same county has several records (rows) - each for a crop type, its area in acres, and the percentage that it takes up in the overall area of the county.
warning Save save your map.

6.4 Pivot Table

In order to visualize the composition of crop types within each county, we need the crop types as individual attributes that is associated to just one county. This is referred to as “flattening one-to-many relationships,” and we use Pivot Table to achieve the below:

  1. In the Geoprocessing pane, search and open the Pivot Table tool
    • Input Table: TabulateCropType
    • Input Field: CountyName
    • Pivot Field: d_MAJORITY
    • Click Run
  2. Once the geoprocessing finishes, a new table TabulateCropType_PivotTable gets added to your Contents. Open the table to see the transposed results.
warning Save save your map.

6.5 Join Field

The crop type information we derived for each county is tabular, with no geometries associated with them. However, we do have geographic information in the table that is unique to each record: the county name. We also have the county geometries portrayed by the IowaCounties layer. To add the tabular data to the counties shapes, we use the county name that they both share.

  1. In the Geoprocessing pane, search and open the Join Field tool
    • Input Table: IowaCounties
    • Input Join Field: CountyName
    • Join Table: TabulateCropType_PivotTable
    • Join Table Field: CountyName
    • Transfer Fields:
      • Edible Crop
      • Feed Crop
      • Non Agriculture
      • Other
    • Click Run
  2. Once the geoprocessing finishes, Open the Attribute Table for IowaCounties and you will see that the crop type attributes are added.

6.7 Raster Calculations

To quantify change in soil carbon, we need two data layers that represent two time periods: soc_t1_30cm.tif (current) and soc_t2_me.tif (future). These two layers are raster data (fields view) as opposed to vector data (discrete objects view) that we have been using so far. The values in each cell/pixel represent soil organic carbon in tons per hectare. Our goal here is to calculate the average change in soil carbon for each county, to see if the composition in crop type has an impact, i.e. less soil carbon in counties that have more commodity crop.

  1. On the top of the interface, click on Analysis tab >> Raster Functions
  2. Under Math, click on Minus
  3. For the value in each cell, the Minus function subtracts the second input from the first, so our inputs are: soc_t2_me.tif - soc_t1_30cm.tif
    • Click Create New Layer
  4. Rename the new raster layer that gets created (Minus_soc_t2_me.tif_soc_t1_30cm.tif) to Change in SOC
